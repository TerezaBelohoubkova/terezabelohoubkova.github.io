Understanding UX maturity in a project

Project

SAP Green Ledger

Roles

Researcher
Facilitator

Timeline

4 months

Method

User interviews

tl;dr

Motivation

Because of restructuring and my new role as a designer at SAP, my colleague Martin and I mapped out what our stakeholders think and expect from UX. Before I came to SAP no designer was assigned to only one project, so development teams werenâ€™t used to working with a designer on a daily basis.
We focused on learning what past experiences our colleagues had, what would be their ideal cooperation with UX and how advanced their skills with Figma are (mostly to build an ideal Figma structure).

Findings

We discovered that stakeholders expect UX to:

be involved early in the development process

ensure consistency across apps and to adhere to guidelines

translate business requirements to feasible and intuitive designs

Impact

The internal research lead to:

a stakeholder workshop on UX discovery to increase UX visibility and find UX allies

better structured Figma file tailored to each stakeholders' needs

Learnings and Takeaways

Remove bias

We were conducting research about our own work with colleagues who'd be working with us in the future. This might have created nuances and untruthful feedback.
However, the stakeholders' answers would repeat, so that was our confirmation of truth.
At that time there was no other way to conduct the research. If I were to do it again, I'd ask a different colleague to conduct the interviews.

It's ok to improvise

During interviews an interviewee would answer in a way that I knew they wouldn't be able to answer the coming questions. I had to improvise and edit the script on the spot to keep the conversation flowing and not make it seem like I was ignoring what the interviewee said. This was a learning to prepare questions in case the answer is not what I need.

Test scales

Part of the research were scales where interviewees would place themselves on a scale from "strongly disagree" to "strongly agree". Issue came when interviewees would have to answer where to place themselves. Because the points on the scale were named only the worst and best, they'd struggle to express their feelings.
After adjusting the scales to have numbers 1 to 5 assigned to the points, interviewees' expressiveness increased. Validation from a fresh eye would prevent this situation from happening.

Using research data

Restructuring in SAP also brought on a big push for customer discovery before development. From our research we knew that stakeholders weren't used to doing user research and expected UX to only deliver mockups.
That's why we took data obtained during the stakeholder interviews and created a workshop for a group of developers and a group of product and development managers. The workshop's goal was to show stakeholders what the discovery process and consequently UX is about.
Division of the group had it's reason: product and development managers would work with anonymized data from developers and developers would work with data of the managers.

Workshop structure

1st session: Affinity Mapping

At the beginning of the workshop, the attendees would get to know a persona of either a developer or product manager to enforce empathy and familiarize them with the concept. The group consumed and clustered research data. Based on the research insights they'd formulate problems in the form of "How might we" statements.

2nd session: Ideation

Based on How might we statements from the 1st session, the attendees created ideas with Crazy 8s method to solve a determined problem. We discussed their ideas and graded them based on chosen criteria, ie. impact, effort, feasibility. A winner of this decision matrix was taken into journey mapping. In the last part of the 2nd session the group was supposed to validate their ideas but due to time reasons, I only showed a question creation guide.

Feedback

After each session I'd ask for feedback from the workshop attendees. Most impactful discussion we had was developers didn't see value in involvement during the discovery phase. They expressed interest in the 2nd part of the double diamond design process (after problem definition) but did not find the problem discovery important to them.
